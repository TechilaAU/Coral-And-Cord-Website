Design Notes

Reading the reef: where our patterns begin.

Design · 4 min read

Every Coral & Cord collection starts with a dive slate, not a mood board. Before anything gets drawn we spend time on the reef the collection is named after - photographing coral structure, watching how fish hold on the current lines, noting the colours the water actually produces at depth.

Back at the desk those references are redrawn by hand as fine line work. We borrow from two traditions that turn out to be natural partners: scientific illustration, with its patient accuracy, and mandala geometry, with its repetition and calm. Coral is geometric by nature. Staghorn branches, plate corals, the growth rings of a bommie - the reef has been drawing mandalas for longer than we have.

Why line work

Loud all-over photo prints are everywhere in fishing apparel. Line work does the opposite - it holds detail at close range and settles into texture at a distance, which is why a Coral & Cord shirt reads as artwork on the boat and as a plain good looking shirt at the pub afterwards.

Each artwork carries the story of one reef. When you wear Ribbon Reef, the tide lines on the sleeve are the ribbons themselves.

Reef Guide

Five reefs worth the run.

Fishing · 5 min read

Ask ten North Queensland skippers for their favourite reef and you will get eleven answers. These five keep coming up, and each one has a Coral & Cord collection named after it or on the way.

Ribbon Reefs

The string of narrow outer reefs north of Cairns. Steep walls, big current, giant trevally and dogtooth on the pressure edges. The pattern on our Ribbon Reef collection follows the shape of these walls.

Osprey Reef

A seamount in the Coral Sea, a serious overnight run from anywhere. A thousand metre drop off the wall and water so clear it feels like flying.

Cod Hole

Home of the famous potato cod at the top of Ribbon Reef Number 10. More a pilgrimage than a fishing spot - take the camera, not the gaff.

Hardy Reef

Off the Whitsundays, with the famous Heart Reef next door. Lagoon systems full of coral trout country.

Whitsunday Passage

Not one reef but a hundred islands of fringing country - protected water when it blows, mackerel highways when it does not.

Technical

UPF 50+ explained: what the rating actually means.

Gear · 3 min read

UPF is to fabric what SPF is to sunscreen, with one important difference - it does not wash off, sweat off or wear off by lunchtime.

A UPF 50+ rating means the fabric allows less than 2 percent of UV radiation through to your skin. It blocks both UVA and UVB, and it keeps blocking them for the life of the garment. On a Queensland summer day when the UV index is extreme by 9am, that difference matters more than any other spec on the shirt.

What to look for

Coverage beats everything - long sleeves, a collar you can stand up, and a buff for the neck, ears and face that a cap misses. Then comfort: if a shirt is not moisture wicking and quick dry you will take it off, and a shirt on the deck protects nobody.

Every piece in every Coral & Cord collection is rated UPF 50+. The reef gives us the artwork; the least we can do is keep you on it longer.
